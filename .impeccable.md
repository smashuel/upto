# Upto — Design Context

## Design Context

### Users
NZ recreationalists and outdoor professionals — anyone heading out on a "skitz mission" (a serious or ambitious outdoor trip). They use the app in two modes: desk planning the night before, or quickly in the car park before heading out. Either way, speed and simplicity are non-negotiable for the creator. On the receiving end, emergency contacts and rescue teams need a clean, navigable map showing intended route and real-time progress — legible under stress.

### Brand Personality
**Rugged. Safe. Precise.**

- **Rugged**: Feels at home in the field. Not fragile, not corporate. Built for people who go outdoors seriously.
- **Safe**: Reassuring, not anxiety-inducing. The app's job is to make people feel like they've done the responsible thing before heading out.
- **Precise**: Data-accurate. What3words locations, GuidePace time estimates, topo-level route info. No vagueness where lives may depend on it.

Voice and tone: direct, efficient, no fluff. Like a well-briefed guide — trustworthy, calm, capable.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Light mode only.** No dark mode.
- **Photography-grounded**: Real NZ outdoor imagery (mountains, fjords, highland mist) as environmental backdrop, not decoration. The app should feel like it belongs in that landscape.
- **Progressive density**: The creation flow is minimal and fast. The map/route view is information-dense — somewhere between a topo map and Strava's mapping UI. Complexity reveals itself only when the user needs it.
- **Anti-references**: 
  - Not an insurance app — no corporate sterility, no anxiety-triggering language, no bureaucratic forms.
  - Not gamified — no badges, streaks, or Strava-style social performance.
  - Not cluttered — traditional topo map UIs overwhelm. Start clean, layer depth on demand.
- **Color**: Deep slate blues, mountain grays, sky blue accent, forest green, golden hour orange — all pulled from the landscape. The palette should feel extracted from a photograph, not invented in a color picker.
- **Typography**: Poppins for headings (weight + authority), Inter for body (legibility at small sizes in dense map UI). No decorative fonts.

### Design Principles

1. **Clarity first, depth on demand.** The base plan should take under 2 minutes to create. Smart track suggestions do the heavy lifting. Route planning and advanced features are opt-in layers, not default complexity.

2. **Safety without bureaucracy.** Every screen should feel reassuring and capable — never clinical, form-heavy, or scary. The goal is confidence, not compliance.

3. **Precision where it counts.** In the map and route views, information density is a feature. Topo-level accuracy, what3words pins, time estimates — but always legible and spatially clear, never chaotic.

4. **Nature-grounded visuals.** The visual language comes from the landscape, not from tech conventions. Photography, terrain, and earth tones anchor the UI in the physical world users are planning to enter.

5. **Confidence by design.** After sending a TripLink, the user should feel: *"I'm good to go. People know where I am."* The receiver should feel: *"I know exactly where they are and what to do."* Every interaction should leave both parties more certain, not less.

## Design Critique Findings (2026-04-04)

Design health score: **20/40 — Acceptable, significant improvements needed.**

### Confirmed Anti-Patterns to Remove
- **Glassmorphism overuse** — `backdrop-filter: blur()` appears 6+ times (card default, step-wizard, map-controls, button tooltip). Reserve for elements literally floating over photography only (hero overlays, map controls). Remove from `.card` default and `.step-wizard`.
- **Identical icon grid** — Activity type selection is 7 cards of icon + heading + description. Mountain icon used for hiking, trail running, AND climbing. Replace with distinct icons; consider grouping or progressive reveal of less common types.
- **Hero metric row in preview** — MapPin/Location + Calendar/Start Date + Clock/Start Time in a three-column stat grid is the AI dashboard template. Redesign for context-appropriate information density.
- **Cards nested in cards** — CreateAdventure uses 3 nesting levels (progress Card → step-wizard → nav Card). Flatten to 1.
- **7+ button color variants** — `fjord`, `highland`, `sunrise`, `sky`, `adventure`, `mountain`, `forest` is not a system. Reduce to: primary, secondary, danger, and ghost.
- **Undefined CSS classes** — `.text-charcoal`, `.text-stone` are used in components but not defined in globals.css. Fix or remove.

### Priority Improvements

#### P1: Remove hero section from Create flow
The 40vh highland photo on `/create` delays the form and contradicts the "under 2 minutes" principle. Users arriving from the car park should land directly in the wizard. Remove entirely; let the form structure communicate capability.

#### P1: Invert public view hierarchy for receivers
PublicAdventureView is opened by emergency contacts and potentially rescue coordinators. Current layout: 50vh photo → title → scroll to data. Required layout: trip title + status → precise location + what3words → high-contrast "Call [Primary Contact]" CTA → then secondary details. The `outline-secondary` style on Call/Email buttons must become the most visually prominent element on the page. The what3words address needs a "use with emergency services" annotation.

#### P2: Simplify step indicator
The 5-step pill row showing title + description for all steps simultaneously is cognitive noise on desktop and illegible on mobile (each pill becomes ~70px). Show only: progress bar + "Step X of 5 — [Current Step Name]". Full step map only on explicit expand.

#### P2: Fix activity icon differentiation
Hiking → Footprints icon. Trail Running → a distinct activity/run icon. Climbing → Mountain icon (keep for this one). Cycling → Bike. Water Sports → Waves. Winter Sports → Snowflake. Other → MapPin. Never reuse the same icon for different activities.

#### P2: Rewrite wizard copy from insurance-speak to outdoor-speak
Replace: "comprehensive safety wizard", "keep your loved ones informed", "Trip safety powered by upto."
Direction: direct, confident, trail-guide tone. "Where are you headed?" not "Location & Route — Where you're going and your planned route." "Who should we call?" not "Emergency Contacts — Who to notify in case of emergency."

#### P2: Add contextual what3words explanation
First appearance of a what3words input must include: a one-line plain-English explanation ("3-word codes pinpoint any 3m² on earth — emergency services use these") and a visible example.

#### P3: Add localStorage draft save to wizard
Form data should be written to localStorage on every step advance. On returning to `/create` with a saved draft, offer "Continue your last trip" option. Prevents data loss for Casey (interrupted mobile user).

#### P3: Remove the Preview button from steps 1–3
Previewing an empty or half-completed form shows "Untitled Trip / TBD / No contacts added" — a bad first impression of the feature. Show Preview only once the minimum required fields are complete (after step 4 at minimum), or only on step 5.

### Voice & Copy Directives
- Never use: "wizard", "comprehensive", "detailed", "loved ones", "in case of emergency" (insurance language)
- Use instead: direct verbs and plain nouns. "Where are you going?", "Add a contact", "Your plan", "Who knows you're out?"
- Status labels should be actionable or descriptive: not "planned" / "active" / "completed" but something that communicates time sensitivity to a receiver
- what3words codes should appear alongside a tap-to-copy button and a "share with rescuers" affordance, not styled as inline `<code>`

### Receiver Experience (Public View) Non-Negotiables
- Critical data must be above the fold on mobile (375px screen) with no scroll required
- Primary emergency contact must have a high-contrast call button as the most prominent interactive element
- Trip status must be visually differentiated for urgency (overdue = red, active = green, planned = neutral)
- what3words address must have a "Copy for rescue services" action
- Hero photography should not appear at all — or at most as a subtle background tint, never as a 50vh block
